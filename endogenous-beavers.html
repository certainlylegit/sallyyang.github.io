ENDOGENOUS BEAVERS

  • by:Sally Yang
  • date: 18 May 2022

The Endogenous Beavers is an economic research-themed parody card game that Grace Choo and I designed in 2020, when we were first-year undergraduates at the LSE.

It is not for sale and in very limited distribution. For you to have a copy of this, I presume you must have helped either of us immensely in some way. Thank you!

This game is a parody of Exploding Kittens. If you are familiar with that, you will learn this easily.

Let’s get started with the game!

SETUP

  1. To start, remove all the Endogenous Beavers from the deck and set them aside.
  2. Remove all of the RCT Cards from the deck and deal 1 to each player.
  3. Insert the extra RCT Cards back in the deck.
  4. Shuffle the remaining deck and deal 4 cards face down to each player.
  5. Insert enough Endogenous Beavers back into the deck so that there is 1 fewer than the number of people playing. Remove any extra Endogenous Beavers from the game.
  6. Shuffle the deck, and put it face down in the middle of the table. This is your Draw Pile. Also, leave some space on the table for a Discard Pile.
  7. Pick a direction (clockwise or anticlockwise), and pick a player to go first.

TAKING YOUR TURN

Gather all of your cards into your hand and look at them. Do one of the following:

PASS

Play no cards.

OR

PLAY

Play a card by placing it face up on top of the Discard Pile and following the instructions on the card.

Some cards will end your turn immediately. Some cards do notーyou can play them as many times as you like without ending your current turn.

End your turn by drawing a card from the top of the Draw Pile into your hand and hoping it’s not an Endogenous Beaver. You can skip this if you have played a Just Assume or a Sabbatical card.

Play then continues around the table. The last player who hasn’t died from an Endogenous Beaver wins the game.

You won’t ever run out of cards in the Draw Pile because you inserted enough Endogenous Beavers to kill all but 1 player.

HOW DOES EACH CARD WORK?

The two most important cards are:

ENDOGENOUS BEAVER

Oh dear, your worst nightmares have come true! Kiss goodbye to your empirical strategy.

You must show this card immediately. Unless you have an RCT card, you’re dead, and you’ll have to discard all of your cards, including the Endogenous Beaver.

RCT

A Randomised Controlled Trial funded generously by the Economics Society Beaver Welfare Division. Allows you to survive one endogeneity attack.

If you drew an Endogenous Beaver you can play this card instead of dying.

Discard the RCT card. Take the Endogenous Beaver, and without reordering or viewing the other cards, secretly put it back in the Draw Pile anywhere you’d like.

If you’d like, hold the deck under the table so that no one else can see where you put it.

The three cards below, when played, will end your turn immediately:

TAKE A SABBATICAL

You take a much-needed break, leaving your coauthor to agonise over the paper.

Do not draw any cards. Instead, immediately force a player of your choice to take one turn. Play then continues from your victim, who takes a turn as per normal.

TAKE A “SABBATICAL”

Your department firmly requests for you to take a break, leaving your coauthor to agonise over the paper.

Same as the Sabbatical card, except that your victim has to take 2 turns instead of just 1.

JUST ASSUME

Immediately end your turn without drawing a card. If you have to take many turns, it skips only one of those turns.

The effect of Sabbaticals and “Sabbaticals” can stack. If the victim of a Sabbatical card plays a Sabbatical card on any of their turns, the new target must take any remaining turns plus the number of attacks on the Sabbatical card just played (e.g. two turns, then three, and so on).

Just Assume is a bit weaker, because one Just Assume card skips only one turn. Playing a Just Assume card as a defence to a “Sabbatical” card only ends one of the two turns you are supposed to take. Two Just Assume cards would end both turns.

The three cards below do not progress your current turn. You can play as many as you want in one turn:

CHECK FOR BALANCE

Privately view the top three cards from the Draw Pile and put them back in the same order. Don’t show the cards to the other players.

NEW DATASET

Shuffle the Draw Pile without viewing the cards until told to stop. (Useful when you know there’s an Endogenous Beaver coming.)

FUNDING CUT

Force any other player to give you one card from their hand. However, that player chooses which card to give you.

HISTORY

Grace and I were stuck at home in Singapore during the summer 2020 lockdown. It was then when we became executive committee members of the LSESU Economics Society. Bored, we began work on a new speaker souvenir for the Society's Amartya Sen Club, a weekly seminar featuring academic economists. A game about economic research felt cool and appropriate.

After weeks of intense literature review (i.e. playing board games), I proposed adapting the play style of Exploding Kittens. I thought that recipients can better recognise our game as a parody if we adapted a popular game. It also helped people learn how to play, though we didn't design the cards expecting people to actually play them. Though game mechanics are not copyrighted, we do not intend to sell this for profit.

Grace supplied the art. I digitised them and sent for printing. The card variety was limited by the budget and our first-year undergraduate-level knowledge of econometrics. Nonetheless, the humble Endogenous Beavers was born!

Sen Club underwent a sudden rise in popularity in the following year. Copies were shipped to Josh Angrist, Paul Milgröm, Gregory Mankiw, etc., though we're unsure whether they managed to reach the US.

If you're wondering why the beavers look so much like cats, a sliiiiight miscommunication between beaver-loving Grace and cat-loving me resulted in the unique beaver-cat amalgamations.
